## Network Analysis (about 300 words)
My network visualizes my personal digital ecosystem by connecting apps and services to parent companies and to the data types they collect or use. The most central nodes in my network are the big companies, especially Google and Apple, because so many everyday tools connect back to them through ownership and account ecosystems. Google links services like Gmail, Drive, Maps, YouTube, and Chrome, which makes it a high-degree node that sits near the center of the network. Apple also becomes central because the iPhone and iCloud connect multiple services and act as an entry point for other apps.

Several clusters show up clearly. One cluster is school and productivity, where Gmail, Drive, Canvas LMS, and Zoom relate through accounts and communication tools. Another cluster is social media, like Instagram and TikTok, which connects strongly to data types like device identifier and messages. A third cluster is shopping and payments, such as Amazon shopping and Apple Pay, which connects to purchase history and profiling. What stands out is how data types behave like bridges. Nodes like browsing history, search history, and advertising profile connect across categories, which suggests that the boundaries between school, entertainment, and shopping are not real boundaries in the data system.

What surprised me is how quickly the network recenters on a few hubs even when the apps feel unrelated. The structure makes convenience look like centralization, where a small number of companies can connect many parts of daily life through data and accounts. The biggest missing part of my network is what I cannot easily see or verify, like third-party trackers, data brokers, ad exchanges, and the behind-the-scenes infrastructure and labor that keep platforms running. Also, representing relationships as simple lines flattens complexity because data collection and sharing can depend on settings, policies, time, and hidden partners.

## Artist Statement (about 250 words)
This network is a map of my everyday digital life, but it is also a map of power. By drawing connections between apps, parent companies, and data types, the visualization shows how a few major platforms become central in ways that are not obvious when I use the apps one by one. When a company sits at the center of many services, it becomes easier for data to move across contexts and for a single profile to form from different parts of life. The network helps make that concentration visible.

Following Drucker’s argument that graphical displays are interpretations rather than neutral facts, I see this network as a constructed model, not a complete truth. I made choices about what counts as a node and what counts as a relationship, and that shapes the story the network tells. Ownership is easy to map, but a lot of the data economy is hidden. Even edges like shares data with can be hard to verify because the most important flows often happen through third parties that users do not see.

The network also shows who is visible and who is invisible. Companies and apps are highly visible because they are branded and easy to name. Users, workers, and communities affected by surveillance, content moderation, gig work, or data center expansion are not present as powerful nodes. If I built a different network focused on data brokers, labor, or physical infrastructure, the structure would look very different, and different actors would become central. That is part of what this assignment made me notice: network visualizations do not just reveal reality, they frame what counts as reality.
