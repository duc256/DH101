## Research Documentation
For this project, I made a digital map of AI infrastructure using three layers: corporate headquarters, data centers, and invisible labor sites tied to content moderation and data labeling. I picked these layers because they show how AI is not just “online” or floating in the cloud. It depends on real places, real resources, and real people. When I put the layers together, a clear pattern showed up. Decision making and profit are concentrated in a few tech hubs, while the physical infrastructure and the human work behind AI are spread across other regions.

For my framework, I used the Data Feminism introduction by Catherine D’Ignazio and Lauren Klein. Their focus on power, and their repeated questions about who is missing and who is doing the work, helped me decide what to map and what to emphasize. I used company location pages and data center community pages to confirm the headquarters and major data center sites. For context on why data centers cluster where they do, I used regional reporting and journalism that discusses factors like available land, tax incentives, strong network connectivity, and limits around energy and water. For the invisible labor layer, I relied on investigative reporting about outsourced moderation and data work, especially the parts that describe working conditions and the emotional impact of safety-related tasks.

The hardest information to find was often the most important information. Exact data center locations can be hard to confirm because companies are not always fully transparent. It was also difficult to track subcontracting chains for moderation and labeling work, or to find consistent public data about worker protections and environmental impacts near specific facilities. That missingness ended up becoming part of my argument, because it shows how power can shape what information becomes visible and what stays hidden. What surprised me most was how polished and available corporate information was compared to the gaps around labor and local impacts. Mapping these layers together helped me see AI less as a product and more as a global system built on infrastructure, policy, and human work.

## Artist Statement
My map argues that AI is not virtual or borderless in the way it is often marketed. It is built through a global geography where power is concentrated, while costs and labor are distributed. By mapping corporate headquarters, data centers, and invisible labor sites together, I wanted to create a counter-map. Instead of repeating the dominant narrative that AI is clean, automated, and everywhere, the map shows the physical and human systems that make AI possible but are easy to ignore.

This project is shaped by Data Feminism, especially the idea that data and data visualization are not neutral. The headquarters layer represents where major decisions get made. The data center layer represents the physical backbone of AI, including land, electricity, cooling, and regulation. The invisible labor layer represents people who do the work that makes AI systems usable and “safe,” like labeling training data and moderating harmful content.

What is missing from the map is part of what it reveals. It was easier to find official information about corporate sites than detailed information about subcontracted labor, worker protections, or environmental burdens near facilities. That gap is not random, and it affects accountability. If a tech executive drew this map, it might highlight innovation hubs and investment. If a moderator, data labeler, or someone living near a data center drew it, the focus might shift toward trauma, resource strain, and who bears the risks.
